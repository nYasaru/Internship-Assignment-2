+ OTHER TIPS

01

If you find yourself struggling to get an idea across, it’s easy to default to cynicism. “They don’t trust my idea”, “They don’t get how I work”. A huge part of design is good communication. If they don’t get you or seem closed minded. Try see it a chance to improve on your communication skills.

02

Take those lunchbreaks with your teammates, take them up on their invites to go get coffee with them. Working is alot easier and a lot more enjoyable when you like the people you work with, so give yourself a chance to get to know them. (Also be prepared to learn self-reliance and other ways of team bonding, in extreme cases. Like COVID)

03

Take the time to learn about the different management styles that exist, once you know that these exist, you can figure out which styles you respond best to and if you’re in a situation where a manager follows a style you aren’t used to or dislike, you can begin to take steps to adapt your working style around that.

- Feedback is your best friend
- Be as honest as professionally possible.
- Try to make “safe” mistakes, it speeds up the learning process.
- Say yes to things but if it’s something brand new to you. Please ask for help

- Before you present your work, make sure you’ve gone through it in detail.
- Try to take it a bit easy. People know you’re an intern. Learn as you go.
- If you mess up, apologize and move on. Dwelling on it helps no one.

LEARNING EXPERIENCES

+ APPLICATION

A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is a product that is good enough to solve the core problems of customers, and only has functions for that.

Why is it important to know what an MVP is? It is easy to get overwhelmed by the scale of a project, especially in my situation after having not produced results for a significant amount of time, learning about the MVP model helped ease alot of tension and guilt.

Instead of focusing on too much at once and failing to achieve any form of progress, by following the MVP model it makes it alot easier to distill a project down to it’s essence and attack it piece-by-piece.

Regardless of your profession the MVP model acts as a great tool to understand a project and being able to execute it can be extremely insightful.

From the lens of a UI/UX designer, it opens up possibilities for early prototypes, early user testing and that can be extremely cost effective and/or save money later down the line for the project when you could uncover problems early in development.

UNDERSTAND
THE
PRODUCTION
TIMELINE

1. DEVELOPMENT
2. PRE - PRODUCTION
3. PRODUCTION
4. POST - PRODUCTION
5. DISTRIBUTION

A principle I learned from my time during video production classes was the 5 step production timeline (As opposed to the 3 step of Pre/Production/Post.) I think as designers or even as creatives it can act as a strong benchmark for planning. From my experience on UI/UX projects, it never starts with a wireframe.

The idea is that you can apply these 5 steps to almost any creative process and break it down to give yourself and/or your team a roadmap of how to get to the finish line. It helps break down herculean tasks into easier to digest pieces.

The first few steps are an opportunity to figure out the scope of the project, which skills will you need to learn, what are the limits of experimentation of the brief, how well is the brief defined? And the other stages offer a sense of structure to a seemingly subjective part of the work, the actual design process.

The final stage helps with contextualizing the value this work may have, what are the end goals for this and being able to have even a rough outline of what that may be helps.
